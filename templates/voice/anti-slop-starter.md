# Anti-Slop Rules

> AI writing has predictable failure modes. These 10 patterns make content sound artificial. Flag them during review. If 3 or more appear in a piece, rewrite from scratch instead of patching.

## How to Use This File

1. After generating content with AI, read it against this checklist.
2. Mark each pattern as present or absent.
3. If 0-2 patterns appear: edit them out individually.
4. If 3+ patterns appear: the content has structural slop. Rewrite with a more specific prompt, not surgical edits.
5. Over time, add patterns specific to your own writing blind spots.

---

## Pattern 1: Em-Dash Overuse

**What it looks like:** "Building in public - while terrifying - changed everything."

**Why it is slop:** AI uses em-dashes as a crutch to insert parenthetical thoughts. Real writing uses periods to end sentences and starts new ones.

**What to do instead:** Split into two sentences. "Building in public changed everything. It was terrifying at first." Or use commas for genuinely short asides.

---

## Pattern 2: Authority Signaling

**What it looks like:** "As a seasoned developer with over a decade of experience..." or "Having worked with hundreds of clients..."

**Why it is slop:** Your credibility comes from what you say, not from announcing your credentials. If the content is good, the reader trusts you. If it is not, no amount of credential-listing helps.

**What to do instead:** Delete the credential. Just make the point. Let your specific knowledge prove the expertise.

---

## Pattern 3: Narrator Setup

**What it looks like:** "Let me tell you about the time I..." or "Here is the thing about..." or "I want to share something with you."

**Why it is slop:** It narrates the act of communicating instead of just communicating. "Let me tell you about X" adds zero information. Just tell them about X.

**What to do instead:** Delete the setup. Start with the actual content. "The deploy broke at 2am" beats "Let me tell you about the time the deploy broke at 2am."

---

## Pattern 4: Dramatic Framing

**What it looks like:** "Everything changed when..." or "That was the moment I realized..." or "What happened next surprised everyone."

**Why it is slop:** Artificial drama. It manufactures emotion instead of earning it through specifics. Real turning points are obvious from the details. You do not need to announce them.

**What to do instead:** State what happened. Let the reader decide if it is dramatic. "We switched from Postgres to SQLite. Deployment time dropped from 45 minutes to 3."

---

## Pattern 5: Parallel Sentence Structure

**What it looks like:** "It is not about the code. It is about the people. It is about the culture. It is about showing up every day."

**Why it is slop:** AI loves repeating the same sentence structure for emphasis. It reads as a motivational poster, not a person writing.

**What to do instead:** Vary sentence length and structure. Mix short declarations with longer explanations. Break the pattern.

---

## Pattern 6: Bookend Summary

**What it looks like:** Opening with a thesis, then ending with "And that is why X matters" or restating the opening point verbatim.

**Why it is slop:** Blog posts are not essays with a conclusion paragraph. Your reader got the point. Restating it signals that you do not trust them.

**What to do instead:** End with the last useful point, a practical next step, or a forward-looking thought. Do not circle back to restate the opening.

---

## Pattern 7: Self-Branded Concepts

**What it looks like:** "I call this the 'Feedback Flywheel'" or "Welcome to what I call 'Intentional Architecture.'"

**Why it is slop:** Naming a concept you just made up gives it false authority. It also reads as personal brand building rather than genuine teaching.

**What to do instead:** Describe the concept without naming it. If the idea is strong, it does not need a brand name. If it is not strong, a brand name will not save it.

---

## Pattern 8: Artificial Urgency

**What it looks like:** "If you are not doing X, you are already behind" or "The window is closing" or "This changes everything."

**Why it is slop:** Manufactured pressure to make content feel important. Readers are not fooled. It reads as marketing copy, not genuine advice.

**What to do instead:** Present the information. Let the reader decide its urgency. "Here is what changed and how it affects your workflow" respects the reader's judgment.

---

## Pattern 9: Colon List Setup

**What it looks like:** "There are three things every developer needs: curiosity, persistence, and a willingness to break things."

**Why it is slop:** AI defaults to this structure constantly. Once you see it, you cannot unsee it. Real writing weaves these points into narrative or uses actual bullet points.

**What to do instead:** If the items deserve a list, use a real bulleted list. If they deserve narrative, write full paragraphs about each. The colon-list is the lazy middle ground.

---

## Pattern 10: Humble Brag Disclaimer

**What it looks like:** "I do not claim to have all the answers, but..." or "This is just my experience, take it with a grain of salt."

**Why it is slop:** False modesty that precedes a confident opinion. The disclaimer is performative. If you are writing it, you think it is worth reading. Own that.

**What to do instead:** Delete the disclaimer. State your position. If you are genuinely uncertain, say what specifically you are uncertain about and why.
