# Anti-Slop Rules - Alex Chen (SaaS Builder)

> These patterns make AI-generated content sound like a LinkedIn influencer instead of a developer who builds things. Flag during review. 3+ flags = rewrite from scratch.

---

## Pattern 1: Em-Dash Parentheticals

**What it looks like:** "The migration - which took three weeks - was worth every hour."

**Why it is slop:** AI inserts em-dash asides constantly. It breaks reading flow and feels artificial after the second occurrence.

**What to do instead:** "The migration took three weeks. Worth every hour." Two sentences. Cleaner.

---

## Pattern 2: Credential Announcement

**What it looks like:** "As someone who has built and shipped four SaaS products..." or "With two years of indie hacking experience..."

**Why it is slop:** If the advice is good, the reader will trust it. If it is not, announcing your resume does not help. Alex's voice earns credibility through specifics, not declarations.

**What to do instead:** Delete the credential. Jump straight to the point. "The billing page needs three things: a price, a button, and a FAQ. Everything else is procrastination."

---

## Pattern 3: The Narrator Voice

**What it looks like:** "I want to talk about something that does not get discussed enough." "Let me share a framework I use."

**Why it is slop:** Narrating the act of writing instead of writing. It adds a sentence that conveys zero information.

**What to do instead:** Delete it. "Pricing psychology matters more than pricing math" beats "I want to talk about something most indie hackers ignore: pricing psychology."

---

## Pattern 4: False Modesty

**What it looks like:** "I am by no means an expert, but..." or "Take this with a grain of salt..."

**Why it is slop:** Alex's voice is direct and opinionated. Hedging before a strong opinion creates tonal whiplash. It reads as performative humility.

**What to do instead:** State the opinion. "Stripe is overkill for most indie SaaS. LemonSqueezy handles 90% of use cases with less code." No disclaimers needed.

---

## Pattern 5: Motivational Escalation

**What it looks like:** "It is not about the code. It is about the impact. It is about the lives you change. It is about building something that matters."

**Why it is slop:** Escalating parallel sentences that build toward an emotional crescendo. Reads like a TED talk transcript, not a developer blog.

**What to do instead:** Say it once. "The code matters less than whether anyone uses it." One sentence. Done.

---

## Pattern 6: The Thesis Restatement

**What it looks like:** Post starts with "Simplicity wins." Post ends with "And that is why simplicity always wins."

**Why it is slop:** The reader does not need the conclusion restated. They read the whole post. Circling back to the opening is a high school essay technique.

**What to do instead:** End with a forward-looking thought or the last practical takeaway. "Next week I am testing whether removing the pricing page entirely increases trial signups."

---

## Pattern 7: Invented Terminology

**What it looks like:** "I call this the 'Revenue-First Architecture' approach." "Welcome to what I call 'Minimum Viable Marketing.'"

**Why it is slop:** Branding a concept gives it false weight. Alex's voice is anti-guru. Making up frameworks contradicts the "just build things and share what works" ethos.

**What to do instead:** Describe the approach without naming it. "Build the billing page before the feature page. If you cannot charge for it, you do not have a product." No brand name needed.

---

## Pattern 8: Dramatic Pivot Moment

**What it looks like:** "Everything changed when I realized..." or "That was the moment everything clicked."

**Why it is slop:** Manufactured narrative drama. Real insights usually arrive gradually. Pretending there was a cinematic moment undermines credibility.

**What to do instead:** State what you learned and when. "After the third failed launch, I started building landing pages before writing code. Conversion rate on the landing page told me whether the idea was worth building."

---

## Pattern 9: Generic Transition Phrases

**What it looks like:** "But here is the thing." "And that brings me to my next point." "Which leads me to..."

**Why it is slop:** Filler transitions that add no information. AI uses them to connect paragraphs. A good paragraph connects to the next one through content, not through a transitional phrase.

**What to do instead:** Delete the transition. Start the next paragraph with its own point. If the connection between paragraphs is not obvious from the content, the structure needs work.

---

## Pattern 10: The Listicle Crutch

**What it looks like:** "Here are the 5 things I learned about pricing." Followed by five one-paragraph sections.

**Why it is slop:** Not all ideas fit neatly into a numbered list. Forcing them into one creates uneven sections where some points get two sentences and others get three paragraphs.

**What to do instead:** If the points are genuinely parallel and roughly equal in depth, a list is fine. If one point is the real insight and the others are supporting, write it as a narrative with that point as the centerpiece.

---

## Alex-Specific Patterns to Watch

### Pattern 11: Over-Comparing to the Old Job

**What it looks like:** "Back at my old company, we would have spent three sprints on this. Now I ship it in a day."

**Why it is slop:** The comparison gets repetitive after a few posts. The reader gets it. Corporate was slow. Indie is fast. Move on.

**What to do instead:** Skip the comparison. Just describe the current approach. The reader can draw their own conclusions.

### Pattern 12: Revenue Humble Brag

**What it looks like:** "It is not about the money, but we crossed $10K MRR last month."

**Why it is slop:** "It is not about X" followed immediately by X. The disclaimer makes the brag more obvious, not less.

**What to do instead:** Share the number directly if it is relevant. "$10K MRR after 14 months. Here is what the growth curve actually looked like." No disclaimer needed. Just context.
