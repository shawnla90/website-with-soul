# 04 - LinkedIn Strategy

LinkedIn rewards a specific format: strong hook, short paragraphs, builder voice, no fluff.

---

## Why LinkedIn Works for Builders

LinkedIn has the fastest feedback loop of any text-based platform. Post something at 8am, you know by 10am whether it landed. The algorithm rewards early engagement heavily, which means your first 20-30 connections who interact with a post determine whether it reaches 200 people or 20,000.

For builders, consultants, and anyone selling expertise, LinkedIn has a unique advantage: the audience is already in work mode. They are thinking about tools, workflows, hiring, and strategy. A post about how you solved a technical problem reaches people who are actively dealing with similar problems.

The catch: LinkedIn's culture penalizes anything that looks like marketing. "Excited to announce" posts get scrolled past. Vulnerability-performance posts ("I got fired and it was the best thing that ever happened to me") are burning out. What works now is showing your work with specificity.

## The Opening Line

The first two lines of your LinkedIn post appear before the "see more" fold on mobile. These lines determine whether someone expands your post or keeps scrolling. They are the most important part of the entire post.

**Rules for openers:**

1. **Lowercase first word.** This signals casual, conversational tone. It breaks the pattern of corporate LinkedIn posts that start with "Excited" or "Thrilled" or "I'm pleased to announce."
2. **Specific, not generic.** "we deleted 14 cloud services nobody was using" beats "here are my top tips for cloud optimization."
3. **Create a gap.** The reader should think "wait, what happened?" or "how did they do that?"
4. **No questions as openers.** "Have you ever wondered...?" is a copywriting cliche. State something interesting instead.

**Example openers that work:**

> spent saturday auditing our AWS bill. found six zombie services.

> the best feature we shipped this quarter took 45 minutes to build.

> a client asked me to rebuild their entire site. I told them to delete 80% of it instead.

> stopped posting on LinkedIn for two months. traffic to my site went up.

**Example openers that do not work:**

> Here are 7 tips for building a better website.

> I'm excited to share that we just launched our new product!

> Have you ever struggled with SEO? I have too. Here's what I learned.

> STOP doing this one thing on your website (you'll thank me later).

## Post Structure

LinkedIn is read on phones. Long paragraphs die on mobile.

**Format rules:**

- Maximum 1-2 sentences per paragraph.
- Single line breaks between paragraphs (LinkedIn collapses double line breaks).
- Total post length: 800-1,300 characters works best. Long enough to say something, short enough to hold attention.
- No bullet points in the main post body (they look cluttered on mobile). Save those for comments.

**Example post structure:**

```
spent two hours this morning fixing a bug that turned out to be a single missing comma.

the error message pointed to the database layer.
the logs pointed to the API.
the actual problem was in a JSON config file I had not touched in 3 months.

lesson: when nothing makes sense, check the files you have not changed.
those are the ones that silently broke during an update you forgot about.

[sign-off]
```

Notice: short paragraphs, whitespace, lowercase energy, a specific story, and a takeaway. No marketing language. No CTA begging for engagement.

## Content Pillars

You need 4-5 recurring content types so you never stare at a blank page wondering what to post.

### 1. Plays and Workflows

Share a specific process you use. Not theory. The actual steps.

> here is exactly how I audit a website in 30 minutes:
>
> 1. run lighthouse. screenshot the scores.
> 2. check core web vitals in search console.
> 3. open the site on a phone over 3G throttling.
> 4. read the homepage out loud. if I stumble, the copy is too complex.
>
> takes 30 minutes. reveals 80% of the problems.

### 2. Building and Sharing

Show what you are working on. Screenshots, metrics, before/after comparisons.

> just shipped a chat widget for our site.
>
> it uses keyword matching to surface relevant content from our blog.
> no expensive AI API calls. just a JSON map of keywords to responses.
>
> cost to run: $0/month.
> took about 4 hours to build.
>
> sometimes the boring solution is the right one.

### 3. Memes and Observations

Light posts that earn engagement and show personality. These balance out the tactical posts.

> client: "can you make the website more modern?"
> me: "what does modern mean to you?"
> client: "you know... modern."
>
> we have all had this conversation.

### 4. Release Reactions

Comment on a new tool, framework update, or industry shift while it is fresh.

> next.js 15 just dropped server actions as stable.
>
> this changes how I think about form handling.
> no more API routes for simple mutations.
> the code reduction is significant.
>
> tried it on a side project last night. initial impression: fast, clean, fewer files.

### 5. Skill Shares

Teach something you know well. Position it as peer-to-peer, not guru-to-student.

> the one SEO concept that changed how I write blog posts:
>
> search intent.
>
> not "what keywords should I target?"
> but "what is the person actually trying to accomplish when they type this?"
>
> when I shifted from keyword-stuffing to answering the actual question,
> my organic traffic tripled in 4 months.

## The Sign-Off

A consistent sign-off at the end of your posts becomes an identity anchor. It is subtle branding that does not feel like branding.

**Examples:**
- Your name or handle
- A short tagline ("building in public")
- A recurring phrase ("ship > plan")

Keep it to one line. Do not make it a paragraph. Do not include a URL. The sign-off is a fingerprint, not a billboard.

## CTA Style

LinkedIn culture has developed antibodies against engagement-bait CTAs.

**What does not work:**
- "Like if you agree!"
- "Comment 'YES' if you want the template!"
- "Share this with someone who needs to hear it."
- "Agree? Disagree? Let me know in the comments!"

**What works:**
- Deliver the value in the post. If people want more, they will ask.
- Drop additional detail in the comments. "Dropping the full list of tools in the comments" gives people a reason to scroll down.
- Ask a genuine question. "What is your approach to this?" works if you actually want to know.

## The Comment Section as a Content Channel

Your own comments on your own post are prime real estate. LinkedIn shows the author's comments prominently.

Use this:

1. Post goes live with the core message.
2. First comment: drop the link to your blog post, tool, or resource. "Full breakdown with code on my site: [link]." This avoids the algorithm penalty for links in the main post.
3. Second comment: add a detail you cut from the main post. This creates engagement and keeps the post in people's feeds.
4. Respond to every comment in the first 2 hours. This signals to the algorithm that the post is generating conversation.

## LinkedIn vs. Your Blog

LinkedIn posts are appetizers. Your blog posts are the meal.

| | LinkedIn Post | Blog Post |
|---|---|---|
| Length | 800-1,300 characters | 1,000-3,000 words |
| Depth | One idea, one angle | Full exploration with examples |
| Lifespan | 48-72 hours of feed visibility | Months to years via SEO |
| Purpose | Drive awareness and engagement | Drive search traffic and conversions |

The play: write the blog post first. Then extract one angle and turn it into a LinkedIn post. Link to the full blog post in your first comment.

## Posting Cadence

**Minimum viable cadence:** 3 posts per week.

**Optimal:** 4-5 posts per week (weekdays only, weekends get lower engagement).

**Best times:** 7-9am in your target audience's timezone. Tuesday through Thursday tend to perform best.

**Consistency matters more than frequency.** Three posts every week beats five posts one week and zero the next.

## Growing From Zero

If you have fewer than 500 connections:

1. Connect with people in your industry. Send a simple connection request with no note (notes get ignored, the acceptance rate is actually higher without them on LinkedIn).
2. Comment on other people's posts. Same rules as Reddit: add specific value, not "great post!"
3. Engage with people who have 5,000-20,000 followers. Their audiences will see your comments. This is the fastest way to get discovered.
4. Post consistently even when nobody engages. The algorithm needs 2-4 weeks of data before it starts distributing your posts meaningfully.

The hardest part is posting into the void for the first few weeks. Push through it. The algorithm catches up.
