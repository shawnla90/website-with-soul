# 01 - Distribution Matrix

Your website is the hub. Social platforms are spokes that drive traffic back to it.

---

## The Core Thesis

You publish on your website first. Always. Then you distribute excerpts, angles, and derivatives to social platforms over the following days. Your blog post is the canonical URL. Everything else is a trailer for it.

This is not a content repurposing strategy. It is an ownership strategy. When a platform changes its algorithm, throttles your reach, or shuts down entirely, your website still exists. Your SEO still works. Your email list still converts.

The platforms are distribution channels. Your website is the product.

## The Four Platforms

Each platform serves a different function. You do not need all four. You need one to start, maybe two within six months.

### LinkedIn

**Best for:** Professional audience, B2B, consulting, SaaS, career builders.

**Mechanics:** Algorithm rewards engagement in the first 60 minutes. Text posts outperform link posts (LinkedIn suppresses external URLs). Comments count more than reactions.

**Content format:** Short paragraphs (1-2 sentences each), heavy whitespace, lowercase openers, strong hook in first two lines before the "see more" fold.

**Link strategy:** Drop the URL in the first comment, not the post body. Reference it naturally: "wrote the full breakdown on my site, link in comments."

**Time to traction:** 2-4 weeks of consistent posting (3-5x/week) before the algorithm picks you up.

### Reddit

**Best for:** SEO amplification, community credibility, long-tail search traffic, LLM retrieval (GEO).

**Mechanics:** Karma-gated posting in most subreddits. Community-driven moderation. Authenticity is currency. Self-promotion gets nuked.

**Content format:** Personal experience posts, mini-tutorials, project breakdowns, "lessons learned" narratives. No marketing language.

**Link strategy:** Provide massive value in the post itself. Link to your blog only when it genuinely adds depth. Subreddit rules vary. Some ban all self-promotion. Read sidebar rules before posting.

**Time to traction:** 2-4 weeks of commenting to build karma. Then 1-2 posts per week.

### X (Twitter)

**Best for:** Builder community, developer audience, real-time commentary, compressed insights.

**Mechanics:** Algorithm rewards replies and quote tweets. Threads get distributed tweet-by-tweet. Single posts with high engagement get pushed to non-followers.

**Content format:** Compressed statements. Each tweet should be self-contained. Threads work for walkthroughs. Single tweets work for observations.

**Link strategy:** Link-in-bio plus occasional direct links. X suppresses external URLs less aggressively than LinkedIn but engagement still drops.

**Time to traction:** Slower than LinkedIn. Expect 4-8 weeks before meaningful follower growth. Reply game matters more than posting frequency.

### Substack / Email

**Best for:** Owned audience, newsletter format, long-form thought leadership, direct relationship.

**Mechanics:** Email lands in inbox. No algorithm. Open rates are your metric, not impressions.

**Content format:** Whatever length serves the idea. Can be 200 words or 2,000. Consistency matters more than length.

**Link strategy:** You own the channel. Link freely to your website, tools, and past content.

**Time to traction:** Slow. 100 subscribers in 3-6 months is realistic without paid growth. But these are high-intent readers.

## Start With One Platform

The biggest mistake is trying to be everywhere on day one. You burn out in two weeks and quit everything.

Pick one platform based on where your audience already spends time:

| Your audience | Start here |
|---|---|
| Hiring managers, B2B buyers, consultants | LinkedIn |
| Developers, indie hackers, technical builders | Reddit or X |
| Writers, thinkers, newsletter readers | Substack |
| Mixed / unsure | LinkedIn (broadest reach, fastest feedback loop) |

Spend 4-6 weeks building rhythm on that one platform before adding a second.

## Cross-Posting Cadence

When you do expand to multiple platforms, stagger your distribution. Never post the same content on the same day across platforms.

**Example cadence for a weekly blog post:**

| Day | Action |
|---|---|
| Monday | Publish blog post on your website |
| Tuesday | LinkedIn post (angle 1, link in comments) |
| Thursday | Reddit post in relevant subreddit (value-first, different angle) |
| Friday or Saturday | X thread (compressed version, 3-5 tweets) |

**Why stagger?**

1. Each platform gets a fresh angle, not a copy-paste.
2. You learn which framing resonates where.
3. You avoid looking like a bot blasting the same message everywhere.
4. Each post can reference the others ("wrote about this on my blog earlier this week" feels natural on Thursday, robotic on Monday).

## Adapt the Angle, Not Just the Format

A blog post titled "How I Cut My Cloud Bill by 60%" becomes:

- **LinkedIn:** "my cloud bill was $840/month. I mass-deleted 14 services I forgot existed. Here is what I found." (storytelling angle, whitespace-heavy)
- **Reddit (r/devops):** "Spent Saturday auditing our AWS bill. Found 6 zombie services. Here is the exact process I used." (tutorial angle, technical detail)
- **X:** "audited our cloud bill. 14 services running that nobody touched in 6 months. deleted them all. saved $500/month. the scariest part: nobody noticed." (compressed narrative, each line a tweet)

Same source material. Three different posts. None of them are copy-paste.

## The Canonical URL Rule

Every social post should eventually drive back to your website. Not with a hard CTA. Not with "check out my blog!" With a natural reference that makes the reader curious enough to click.

Phrases that work:
- "Full breakdown with code examples on my site, link in comments."
- "Wrote the extended version of this with screenshots. Link in bio."
- "This started as a blog post I wrote last week. The long version has the actual configs."

Phrases that do not work:
- "Read my blog post here!"
- "New post alert! Link below."
- "If you enjoyed this, subscribe to my newsletter!"

The first set gives a reason to click. The second set just asks for attention.

## Measuring What Matters

Track these per platform:

| Metric | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Referral traffic to your website | The whole point. Check Google Analytics or Plausible. |
| Engagement rate | Are people actually reading, or just scrolling past? |
| Follower growth rate | Slow and steady beats a viral spike that fades. |
| Content-to-effort ratio | How long does it take you to create a post? If LinkedIn takes 15 minutes and X takes 45, lean into LinkedIn. |

Do not track vanity metrics (total followers, total impressions) without context. 500 engaged followers who click through to your site are worth more than 50,000 passive followers who never leave the platform.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

**Month 1:** Pick one platform. Post 3-4 times per week. Publish one blog post per week on your website. Distribute each blog post to your chosen platform 1-2 days after publishing.

**Month 2-3:** Add a second platform. Cross-post with staggered timing. Refine your angles based on what got engagement in month 1.

**Month 4-6:** You now have a rhythm. Your website has 10-20 blog posts. Your social presence drives consistent referral traffic. Consider adding platform three or starting an email list.

The goal is not to be everywhere. The goal is to be consistently useful in the places your audience already gathers, and to always drive that attention back to something you own.
