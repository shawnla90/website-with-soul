# 02 - Anti-Slop Patterns

AI-generated content has a signature. This chapter teaches you to recognize it and eliminate it before publishing.

## The Problem

Language models produce fluent text that reads well on first pass. The issue is that it all reads the same way. After a few months of reading AI-assisted content, your audience develops pattern recognition. They can feel when something was generated even if they can't articulate why.

These patterns are called "slop." Not because the writing is bad, but because it's generic. It lacks the rough edges, weird choices, and specific details that make human writing feel real.

## The 14 Anti-Slop Patterns

These are the most common tells. Build a checklist from them and run every piece of content through it before publishing.

### 1. Em-dash Overuse

AI loves em-dashes. It uses them where a period, comma, or restructured sentence would work better. One em-dash per article is fine. Three or more and you're broadcasting "AI wrote this."

**Slop:** "The framework is powerful — and surprisingly easy to learn — which makes it ideal for startups."

**Fixed:** "The framework is powerful and surprisingly easy to learn. That makes it ideal for startups."

### 2. Authority Signaling

Phrases that exist to establish credibility instead of demonstrating it. The writing tries to convince you it knows what it's talking about instead of just showing you.

**Slop:** "As someone who has spent years in the trenches of content marketing, I can tell you that..."

**Fixed:** "Here's what actually works."

If your content is good, the authority is self-evident. You don't need a preamble.

### 3. Narrator Setups

Opening a section by telling the reader what you're about to tell them. This burns a sentence (or paragraph) saying nothing.

**Slop:** "Let's explore the key strategies that can transform your content workflow and deliver measurable results."

**Fixed:** Just start with the first strategy. The reader can see what you're doing.

### 4. Dramatic Framing

Inflating the stakes of a mundane topic. Not everything is a revolution, paradigm shift, or game-changer.

**Slop:** "In an era where content is king and attention is the ultimate currency, your website strategy could make or break your entire business."

**Fixed:** "Your website is where people decide if they trust you. The writing matters."

### 5. Three Parallel Sentences

AI defaults to groups of three. Three benefits. Three examples. Three reasons. This pattern appears so frequently in generated text that it's become a reliable tell.

**Slop:** "It's faster. It's cheaper. It's more reliable."

**Fixed:** "It's faster and cheaper. The reliability improvement surprised us most." Break the rhythm. Real writing doesn't come in triplets.

### 6. Bookend Summaries

Opening with a summary, delivering the content, then closing with a near-identical summary. This is essay structure from middle school. Your audience doesn't need a recap of the 500 words they just read.

**Slop:** (Ending) "In summary, by following these five steps, you can build a content pipeline that scales with your business while maintaining authentic voice."

**Fixed:** End with a specific next action or an honest reflection. Or just end. Not everything needs a bow on top.

### 7. Self-Branded Concepts

Inventing proper-noun frameworks for basic ideas. Capitalizing common concepts to make them sound proprietary.

**Slop:** "The Content Velocity Matrix(tm)" or "What I call the Three Pillars of Digital Presence"

**Fixed:** Just explain the idea. If it's genuinely novel, other people will name it for you.

### 8. Artificial Drama

Creating tension that doesn't exist. "But here's the thing..." and "What most people don't realize..." when the following statement is common knowledge.

**Slop:** "But here's what nobody talks about: consistency matters more than virality."

**Fixed:** "Consistency matters more than virality." Everyone talks about this. Just say it.

### 9. Colon-Listed Everything

AI structures nearly every explanation as "Thing: explanation. Thing: explanation. Thing: explanation." Real writing mixes formats. Sometimes a paragraph. Sometimes a quick list. Sometimes a code block.

**Slop:** "Speed: Deploy in under 5 minutes. Cost: Completely free. Flexibility: Works with any framework."

**Fixed:** Write about each point in whatever format fits the point. Maybe speed deserves a paragraph. Maybe cost is a one-liner. Let the content dictate the format.

### 10. Humble Brag Disclaimers

False modesty before making a claim. "I'm no expert, but..." followed by an expert-level assertion.

**Slop:** "I'm certainly not the first person to say this, but I believe that static site generators have fundamentally changed the game."

**Fixed:** "Static site generators changed how we build content sites." If you believe it, just say it.

### 11. Engagement Bait

Ending with generic questions designed to generate comments rather than add value.

**Slop:** "What's your experience with content pipelines? I'd love to hear your thoughts in the comments below!"

**Fixed:** End with a specific, genuine question. Or don't end with a question at all.

### 12. Bullets for Arguments

Using bullet lists where a flowing paragraph would make a stronger argument. Bullets work for reference material, steps, and options. They weaken persuasive writing by removing the connective tissue between ideas.

**Slop:**
```
Why markdown matters:
- It's portable
- It's version-controlled
- It's readable without rendering
- It works everywhere
```

**Fixed:** "Markdown matters because your content stays portable. You can version-control it with git, read it raw without rendering, and move it between platforms without conversion."

### 13. False Dichotomies

Presenting two options as if they're the only choices, then picking the "obvious" one.

**Slop:** "You can either spend thousands on a CMS or build with free tools. The choice is clear."

**Fixed:** "There's a spectrum between expensive CMS platforms and free tools. This playbook sits at the free end because that's where most people should start."

### 14. Bold Headers as Transitions

Using bold text or headers as transition phrases instead of section titles. Every bold line should be scannable on its own.

**Slop:** **"But that's not all."** or **"Here's where it gets interesting."**

**Fixed:** If the next section is about SEO, the header should be "SEO" or "How Search Fits In." Descriptive, not dramatic.

## Building Your Own Checklist

Start with these 14 patterns. Then add your own. The process:

1. Write or generate a piece of content
2. Read it out loud (this catches more slop than silent reading)
3. Mark every sentence that triggers a pattern
4. Rewrite the flagged sentences
5. If a new pattern emerges that isn't on the list, add it

Over time, your checklist becomes specific to your voice. Maybe you never use three-sentence parallels but you tend toward bookend summaries. The checklist should reflect your actual failure modes, not a generic list.

## The Validation Pass

Before publishing any content, run this pass:

```
For each paragraph:
  1. Does it contain information the reader needs? (If no, cut it.)
  2. Does it sound like something I would actually say? (If no, rewrite it.)
  3. Does it trigger any anti-slop pattern? (If yes, fix it.)
  4. Does it contain a specific detail, example, or experience? (If no, add one.)
```

A piece with 3 or more slop flags needs a full rewrite, not a patch. Patching individual sentences in slop-heavy content produces Frankenstein prose. Start the section over with your voice file open.

## The Threshold Rule

Not every piece of content will be zero-slop. That's fine. The goal is awareness, not perfection. Here are reasonable thresholds:

- **Blog posts:** 0-1 flags acceptable. These represent you at your best.
- **Social posts:** 0 flags. These are short enough to get right.
- **Documentation:** 2-3 flags acceptable. Clarity matters more than voice here.
- **Email newsletters:** 0-1 flags. These go to people who already trust you.

## How This Grows

Your anti-slop checklist is a living document. Add patterns when you catch them. Remove patterns that stop appearing. Review quarterly alongside your voice DNA file.

The combination of voice DNA (what you sound like) and anti-slop (what you don't sound like) gives you a complete content quality system. Voice DNA tells AI what to aim for. Anti-slop tells it what to avoid.
